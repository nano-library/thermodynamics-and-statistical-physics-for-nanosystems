Consider a monoatomic ideal gas of \(N\) atoms. For a single atom, the logarithm of the single-particle partition function is:
✦ Single-particle \({\color{#F08FB0}{\ln}}Z\)
✦
\({\color{#F08FB0}{\ln}} Z\) \(=\) \({\color{#F08FB0}{\ln}}V\) \(-\) \(3\) \(2\) \({\color{#F08FB0}{\ln}}\beta + \text{constant}\)
The average energy of a single atom follows from \(\langle E \rangle = -\frac{\partial}{\partial \beta} \ln Z\):
✦ Average energy per atom
✦
\(\langle E \rangle\) \(=\) \(\overline{E}\) \(=\) \(-\) \(\partial\) \(\partial \beta\) \({\color{#F08FB0}{\ln}}Z\) \(=\) \(3\) \(2\) \(kT\)
For \(N\) atoms, the total partition function \(Z_{\text{total}}\) is the product of individual partition functions if the particles were distinguishable:
✦ Product of individual partition functions
✦
\(Z_{\text{total}}\) \(=\) \(Z_1 Z_2 \cdots Z_N\) \(=\) \(Z^N\)
✦ Translational partition function
✦
\(Z_{\text{total}}\) \(=\) \(V\) \(h^3\) \((2 \pi m k T)^{3/2}\)
✦ Logarithm of total partition function
✦
\({\color{#F08FB0}{\ln}}Z_{\text{total}}\) \(=\) \(N \left[ {\color{#F08FB0}{\ln}}V - \frac{3}{2} {\color{#F08FB0}{\ln}}\beta + \text{constant} \right]\)
✦ Average energy from total partition function
✦
\(\langle E \rangle\) \(=\) \(-\) \(\partial {\color{#F08FB0}{\ln}}Z_{\text{total}}\) \(\partial \beta\)
✦ Entropy of ideal gas (uncorrected)
✦
\(S\) \(=\) \(k \left( {\color{#F08FB0}{\ln}}Z_{\text{total}} + \beta \langle E \rangle \right)\)
Substituting \(\ln Z_{\text{total}}\) and \(\langle E \rangle = \frac{3}{2} N k T = \frac{3}{2} N / \beta\):
✦ Substitution step 1
✦
\(S\) \(=\) \(k \left[ {\color{#F08FB0}{\ln}}Z_{\text{total}} + \beta \cdot \frac{3}{2} N \cdot \frac{1}{\beta} \right]\)
✦ Substitution step 2
✦
\(S\) \(=\) \(k \left[ {\color{#F08FB0}{\ln}}Z_{\text{total}} + \frac{3}{2} N \right]\)
✦ Entropy in terms of \(V\) and \(T\)
✦
\(S\) \(=\) \(k \left[ N \left( {\color{#F08FB0}{\ln}}V - \frac{3}{2} {\color{#F08FB0}{\ln}}\frac{1}{kT} \right) + \frac{3}{2} N \right]\)
✦ Expanding the logarithm
✦
\(S\) \(=\) \(k \left[ N \left( {\color{#F08FB0}{\ln}}V + \frac{3}{2} {\color{#F08FB0}{\ln}}k + \frac{3}{2} {\color{#F08FB0}{\ln}}T + c \right) + \frac{3}{2} N \right]\)
✦ Collecting terms
✦
\(S\) \(=\) \(k \left[ N {\color{#F08FB0}{\ln}}V + \frac{3}{2} N {\color{#F08FB0}{\ln}}T + \text{constant} \right]\)
Thus the uncorrected entropy of an ideal gas is:
✦ Uncorrected entropy
✦
\(S\) \(=\) \(k N \left[ {\color{#F08FB0}{\ln}}V + \frac{3}{2} {\color{#F08FB0}{\ln}}T + \text{constant} \right]\)
Now consider what happens when the entire volume is divided into two equal halves by a partition. Let the entropy of each half be \(S_1\) and \(S_2\).
✦ Entropy of left half
✦
\(S_1\) \(=\) \(k N\) \(2\) \(\left[ {\color{#F08FB0}{\ln}}\frac{V}{2} + \frac{3}{2} {\color{#F08FB0}{\ln}}T + \text{constant} \right]\)
✦ Entropy of right half
✦
\(S_2\) \(=\) \(k N\) \(2\) \(\left[ {\color{#F08FB0}{\ln}}\frac{V}{2} + \frac{3}{2} {\color{#F08FB0}{\ln}}T + \text{constant} \right]\)
The total entropy after inserting the partition is \(S_{\text{total}} = S_1 + S_2\). We expect it to equal the original entropy.
✦ Total entropy after partition
✦
\(S_{\text{total}}\) \(=\) \(k N \left[ {\color{#F08FB0}{\ln}}V - {\color{#F08FB0}{\ln}}2 + \frac{3}{2} {\color{#F08FB0}{\ln}}T + \text{constant} \right]\)
The difference between the original entropy \(S\) (before partition) and \(S_{\text{total}}\) (after partition) is:
✦ Entropy difference
✦
\(\Delta S = S - S_{\text{total}}\) \(=\) \(k N {\color{#F08FB0}{\ln}}2\)
We expect \(\Delta S = 0\), but we obtain \(\Delta S = k N \ln 2\). This contradiction is known as the Gibbs paradox.

Resolution of the Gibbs Paradox

The error arises from assuming \(Z_{\text{total}} = Z^N\), which overcounts permutations of indistinguishable particles. The correct partition function includes a factor of \(1/N!\):
✦ Corrected total partition function
✦
\(Z'_{\text{total}}\) \(=\) \(Z^N\) \(N!\)
Using Stirling’s approximation \(\ln N! \approx N \ln N - N\), the corrected entropy becomes extensive and the partition paradox disappears:
✦ Corrected entropy (Sackur-Tetrode)
✦
\(S_{\text{corrected}}\) \(=\) \(k N \left[ {\color{#F08FB0}{\ln}}\left( \frac{V}{N} \right) + \frac{3}{2} {\color{#F08FB0}{\ln}}T + \text{constant} \right]\)
Repeating the two-halves calculation with the corrected entropy gives:
✦ Entropy of each half (corrected)
✦
\(S_1\) \(=\) \(k \frac{N}{2} \left[ {\color{#F08FB0}{\ln}}\left( \frac{V/2}{N/2} \right) + \frac{3}{2} {\color{#F08FB0}{\ln}}T + \text{const} \right]\)
✦ Entropy of each half (corrected)
✦
\(S_1\) \(=\) \(k \frac{N}{2} \left[ {\color{#F08FB0}{\ln}}\left( \frac{V}{N} \right) + \frac{3}{2} {\color{#F08FB0}{\ln}}T + \text{const} \right]\)
✦ Total corrected entropy after partition
✦
\(S_{\text{total}}' = S_1 + S_2\) \(=\) \(k N \left[ {\color{#F08FB0}{\ln}}\left( \frac{V}{N} \right) + \frac{3}{2} {\color{#F08FB0}{\ln}}T + \text{const} \right]\)
✦ Entropy difference vanishes
✦
\(\Delta S = S_{\text{original}} - S_{\text{total}}' = 0\)
Mathematically, permuting identical particles is allowed, but physically it corresponds to the same microstate. The \(1/N!\) factor corrects the overcounting and resolves the Gibbs paradox.
