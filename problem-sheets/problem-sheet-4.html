✦ Problem 1 quantum harmonic oscillator
Problem

A single particle is confined in a one-dimensional quantum harmonic oscillator potential and is in thermal equilibrium with a heat reservoir at temperature \(T\).

(a) Write down the canonical partition function \(Z\).
(b) Calculate the mean energy \(E\).
(c) Find the limiting value of \(E\) for \(kT \gg \hbar\omega\).
Solution
(a) Partition function
Energy levels \(E_n = \hbar\omega\left(n+\frac{1}{2}\right)\), \(n=0,1,2,\dots\)
\[ Z = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} e^{-\beta E_n} \] \[ Z = e^{-\beta\hbar\omega/2} \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \left(e^{-\beta\hbar\omega}\right)^n \] \[ Z = \frac{e^{-\beta\hbar\omega/2}}{1 - e^{-\beta\hbar\omega}} \] \[ Z = \frac{1}{2\sinh \left(\frac{\beta\hbar\omega}{2} \right)}. \]
(b) Mean energy
\[ E = -\frac{\partial \ln Z}{\partial\beta} \] \[ E = \frac{\hbar\omega}{2} + \frac{\hbar\omega}{e^{\beta\hbar\omega}-1}. \]
(c)High-temperature limit (\(kT \gg \hbar\omega\))

For \(\beta\hbar\omega \ll 1\):

\[ e^{\beta\hbar\omega}-1 \approx \beta\hbar\omega \]

Then,

\[ E \approx \frac{\hbar\omega}{2} + \frac{1}{\beta} \] \[ E = \frac{\hbar\omega}{2} + kT \] \[ E \approx kT. \]
✦ Problem 2 half-spin & work
Problem

A spin-\(\frac{1}{2}\) system with magnetic moment \(\mu_0\) is placed in a magnetic field \(B\). Initially, the probabilities are

\[ P_+ = 0.9,\; P_- = 0.1 \]

The system is thermally insulated, and the field is suddenly changed from \(B\) to \(B_0\). After the change,

\[ P_+ = 0.7,\; P_- = 0.3 \]
(a) Calculate the initial and final mean energies.
(b) Determine the work done on the system.
Solution
(a)Mean energies
Energy eigenvalues: \[ E_+ = -\mu_0 B \] \[ E_- = +\mu_0 B \] Initial: \[ \langle E_i \rangle = P_+(-\mu_0 B) + P_-(\mu_0 B) \] \[ \langle E_i \rangle = 0.9(-\mu_0 B) + 0.1(\mu_0 B) \] \[ \langle E_i \rangle = -0.8\,\mu_0 B \] Final: \[ \langle E_f \rangle = 0.7(-\mu_0 B_0) + 0.3(\mu_0 B_0) \] \[ \langle E_f \rangle = -0.4\,\mu_0 B_0 \]
(b) Work done
Since the process is sudden (thermally insulated, no heat exchange), the work done equals the change in internal energy:.
\[ W = \langle E_f \rangle - \langle E_i \rangle \]

Then,

\[ W = (-0.4\,\mu_0 B_0) - (-0.8\,\mu_0 B) \] \[ W = 0.8\,\mu_0 B - 0.4\,\mu_0 B_0. \] \[ W = 0.4 \mu_0 (B-B_0) \]
✦ Problem 3 Gibbs paradox
Problem

For a classical ideal gas of \(N\) identical particles, the canonical partition function is \(Z = (Z_1)^N\) with \(Z_1 \propto V / \beta^{3/2}\).

(a) Show that the entropy is not extensive when the volume \(V\) and number of particles \(N\) are halved.
(b) Explain Gibbs' paradox and how it is resolved.
Solution
(a) Non-extensivity without the \(1/N!\) factor

The given partition function is \(Z = (Z_1)^N\) with \(Z_1 = c\, V / \beta^{3/2}\) (where \(c\) is a constant).
Using the thermodynamic relation \(S = k \bigl( \ln Z + \beta \langle E \rangle \bigr)\), we first compute \(\langle E \rangle = -\frac{\partial}{\partial\beta} \ln Z\).

\[ \ln Z = N \ln Z_1 = N \left( \ln V - \frac{3}{2}\ln\beta + \ln c \right) \] \[ \langle E \rangle = -\frac{\partial}{\partial\beta} \ln Z = N \cdot \frac{3}{2\beta} = \frac{3}{2} N k T \] Then \[ S = k \left[ N \left( \ln V - \frac{3}{2}\ln\beta + \ln c \right) + \beta \cdot \frac{3}{2} N k T \right] \] \[ S = Nk \left( \ln V - \frac{3}{2}\ln\beta + \ln c + \frac{3}{2} \right) \] Since \(\beta = 1/(kT)\), the term \(-\frac{3}{2}\ln\beta = \frac{3}{2}\ln(kT)\). Thus \[ S = Nk \left( \ln V + \frac{3}{2}\ln T + \text{const} \right) \]

The key point: \(S\) contains a term \(Nk \ln V\). Now halve both \(V \to V/2\) and \(N \to N/2\). The entropy of the halved system is

\[ S' = \frac{N}{2}k \left( \ln(V/2) + \frac{3}{2}\ln T + \text{const} \right) \]

If entropy were extensive, we would have \(S = 2S'\). But

\[ 2S' = Nk \left( \ln(V/2) + \frac{3}{2}\ln T + \text{const} \right) \] \[ 2S' = Nk \left( \ln V - \ln 2 + \frac{3}{2}\ln T + \text{const} \right) \]

Comparing with \(S = Nk \left( \ln V + \frac{3}{2}\ln T + \text{const} \right)\), we see

\[ 2S' = S - Nk \ln 2 \quad \Rightarrow \quad S \neq 2S' \]

The entropy is not extensive by an amount \(Nk \ln 2\).

(b) Gibbs paradox and its resolution
Gibbs paradox: When two identical ideal gases at the same temperature and pressure are mixed, classical statistical mechanics (without correct counting) predicts an entropy increase \(\Delta S = Nk \ln 2\) even if the gases are indistinguishable. This contradicts the fact that mixing identical substances should produce no entropy change.
The resolution is to divide the partition function by \(N!\) (correct Boltzmann counting), which accounts for the indistinguishability of particles. The correct canonical partition function is \[ Z = \frac{1}{N!} \left( \frac{V}{\lambda^3} \right)^N \] where \(\lambda\) is the thermal de Broglie wavelength. Using \(S = k(\ln Z + \beta\langle E\rangle)\) with this \(Z\) gives \[ S = Nk \left[ \ln\left( \frac{V}{N\lambda^3} \right) + \frac{5}{2} \right] \] which is extensive (each term scales with \(N\)). The mixing entropy for identical gases becomes zero, resolving the paradox.
✦ Problem 4 thermal de Broglie wavelength & grand canonical ensemble
Problem
(a) Define the thermal de Broglie wavelength and, by comparing it with the mean interparticle spacing, obtain the condition for the validity of classical statistics.
(b) What is a grand canonical ensemble? Obtain the expression for the probability of a microstate and hence define the grand canonical partition function.
Solution
(a) Thermal de Broglie wavelength & classical condition

The thermal de Broglie wavelength is defined as

\[ \lambda_{\text{th}} = \frac{h}{\sqrt{2\pi m kT}} = \frac{h}{p_{\text{thermal}}}, \]

where \(h\) is Planck's constant, \(m\) the particle mass, \(k\) Boltzmann's constant, and \(T\) the temperature. It represents the typical quantum wavelength of a particle with thermal momentum \(\sqrt{2\pi m kT}\).

The mean interparticle spacing is \(d = (V/N)^{1/3} = n^{-1/3}\), where \(n = N/V\) is the number density.
Classical statistics (Maxwell-Boltzmann) is valid when quantum effects are negligible, i.e., when the thermal wavelength is much smaller than the average distance between particles:

\[ \lambda_{\text{th}} \ll d \quad\Rightarrow\quad \frac{h}{\sqrt{2\pi m kT}} \ll n^{-1/3}. \]

Squaring and rearranging gives the condition

\[ n\,\lambda_{\text{th}}^3 \ll 1. \]

The quantity \(n\lambda_{\text{th}}^3\) is the quantum concentration; when it is \(\ll 1\), the gas is dilute and hot enough to obey classical statistics.

(b) Grand canonical ensemble

The grand canonical ensemble describes an open system in thermal and chemical equilibrium with a reservoir. The system can exchange both energy and particles with the reservoir, so its temperature \(T\) and chemical potential \(\mu\) are fixed.
The probability of finding the system in a particular microstate \(s\) with energy \(E_s\) and particle number \(N_s\) is given by

\[ P_s = \frac{1}{\mathcal{Z}} \, e^{-\beta(E_s - \mu N_s)}, \qquad \beta = \frac{1}{kT}. \]

The normalization factor \(\mathcal{Z}\) is the grand canonical partition function (or grand sum):

\[ \mathcal{Z} = \sum_{s} e^{-\beta(E_s - \mu N_s)} = \sum_{N=0}^{\infty} \sum_{\{s \text{ with } N_s=N\}} e^{-\beta(E_s - \mu N)}. \] \[ \mathcal{Z} = \sum_{N=0}^{\infty} \sum_{\{s \text{ with } N_s=N\}} e^{-\beta(E_s - \mu N)}. \] Equivalently, \[ \mathcal{Z} = \sum_{N=0}^{\infty} e^{\beta\mu N} Z_N, \]

where \(Z_N\) is the canonical partition function for \(N\) particles. The grand potential is \(\Phi = -kT \ln \mathcal{Z}\), and average quantities follow from derivatives.
