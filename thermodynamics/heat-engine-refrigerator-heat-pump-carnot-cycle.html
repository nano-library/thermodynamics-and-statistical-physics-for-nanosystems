✨ We shift our focus to the practical applications of thermodynamics, exploring how energy is transferred and converted in real-world devices such as heat engines, refrigerators, and heat pumps, all grounded in the idealized Carnot cycle.
3.1

Heat Engine

🌸
Definition
A heat engine is a device that extracts heat from a high-temperature reservoir, converts part of it into mechanical work, and rejects the remaining heat to a low-temperature reservoir.
Examples: Steam engines (trains), internal combustion engines (cars), external combustion engines, gas turbines.
3.2

Working Principle of Heat Engine (Cyclic Process)

A working substance (for example, water in a steam engine) undergoes a cyclic process:

Liquid \(\rightarrow\) Vapour \(\rightarrow\) Expansion (drives turbine) \(\rightarrow\) Condensation \(\rightarrow\) Back to liquid.

Since the process is cyclic, the system returns to its initial state. Therefore, internal energy change over one complete cycle is:

✦ Equation (3.1)
\(\Delta\) Ucycle = 0

From the First Law of Thermodynamics:

✦ Equation (3.2)
\(\Delta\) U = Q - W

Substituting Equation (3.1), we get

✦ Equation (3.3)
0 = Qnet - Wnet

For a complete cycle:

✦ Equation (3.4)
Wnet = Qnet

If the engine absorbs heat \(Q_h\) from the hot reservoir and rejects heat \(Q_c\) to the cold reservoir, then

✦ Equation (3.5)
Qnet = Qh - Qc
✦ Equation (3.6)
W = Qh - Qc
3.3

Efficiency of a Heat Engine

Thermal efficiency is defined as the ratio of useful work output to heat absorbed from the hot reservoir:

✦ Equation (3.7)
e = W Qh

Substituting Equation (3.6),

✦ Equation (3.8)
e = Qh - Qc Qh

Simplifing the Equation (3.8), we get the thermal efficiency equation

✦ Equation (3.9)
e = 1 - Qc Qh
3.4

Important Observations

Impossible Case
If \(Q_c = 0\), then \(e = 1\), meaning 100% efficiency, which is impossible by the Second Law.
Always Less Than 1
Since \(Q_c > 0\) always, efficiency is always less than 1, i.e., \(e < 1\).
Improving Efficiency
Efficiency increases if \(Q_h\) increases or \(Q_c\) decreases.
3.5

Refrigerator

🌸
Definition
A refrigerator removes heat from a cold reservoir and rejects it to a hot reservoir by using external work. The heat removed from the cold reservoir is \(Q_c\).
3.6

Coefficient of Performance (COP) of Refrigerator

✦ Equation (3.10)
kR = Qc W

Since for a complete cycle, from Equation (3.6),

✦ Equation (3.11)
kR = Qc Qh - Qc
3.7

Heat Pump

🌸
Definition
A heat pump absorbs heat from a cold reservoir and delivers it to a hot reservoir by using external work. The heat delivered to the hot reservoir is (\(Q_h)\).
3.8

Coefficient of Performance (COP) of Heat Pump

✦ Equation (3.12)
kP = Qh W

Using Equation (3.6),

✦ Equation (3.13)
kP = Qh Qh - Qc
3.9

Relation Between COPs of Heat Pump & Refrigerator

✦ Equation (3.14)
kP = Qh W = Qc + W W = Qc W + 1 = kR + 1
3.10

Carnot Engine

🌸
Definition
The Carnot cycle is an idealized thermodynamic cycle that operates between two heat reservoirs at constant temperatures. It represents the most efficient possible cycle, consisting of two isothermal and two adiabatic processes.
3.11

Deriving Efficiency of Carnot Engine

✦ Equation (3.7)
e = W Qh

For a cyclic process, the system returns to its initial state, so the change in internal energy over one complete cycle is zero,

✦ Equation (3.1)
\(\Delta\) Ucycle = 0
✦ First Law for a Cycle
0 = Q net - W \(\Rightarrow\) W = Q net

Substituting Equation (3.6),

✦ Equation (3.8)
e = Qh - Qc Qh

Simplifing the Equation (3.8), we get the thermal efficiency equation

✦ Equation (3.9)
e = 1 - Qc Qh
3.12

Carnot Engine, Reversible Cycle Heat Ratio

For a reversible Carnot cycle, heat transfers occur isothermally — \(Q_h\) absorbed at \(T_h\) and \(Q_c\) rejected at \(T_c\). For any reversible cycle, the ratio of heat transfers equals the ratio of temperatures,

✦ Equation (3.15)
Qc Qh = Tc Th
✦ Equation (3.16)
ecarnot = 1 - Tc Th

This is the maximum possible efficiency for any engine operating between two reservoirs at temperatures \(T_h\) and \(T_c\).
