'Thermodynamical system' (or 'system')
Whatever macroscopic region in space or quantity of matter we select from the Universe for study and analysis, a key aspect of thermodynamics is understanding how energy interactions occur within these systems.

"Macroscopic" here means made of a large number of particles, \(N\), i.e., \(N \geq \mathcal{N}_A \simeq 6.02 \times 10^{23}\), with \(\mathcal{N}_A\) the Avogadro number. This is very important as some properties (eg. temperature) do not apply for single particles.

Types of Systems

Closed System

A system that exchanges energy but not matter with its surroundings. The mass remains constant while heat and work can cross the boundary.

Open System

A system that exchanges both energy and matter with its surroundings. Mass can flow in and out across the boundary.

Isolated System

A system that exchanges neither energy nor matter with its surroundings. No heat, work, or mass crosses the boundary.

Adiabatic System

A system that exchanges no heat with its surroundings, though work can still be transferred. The boundary is thermally insulated.
