✨ The partition function is the sum over all possible microstates of a system, where each state contributes less the higher its energy. It acts as a normalizing factor for probabilities and links the microscopic details to macroscopic properties like free energy.

Let \(\Omega(E)\), \(\Omega '(E^{'})\) be the number of the microstates of \(A\) and \(A^{'}\) respectively.
Since \(A^{*}\) is isolated, \(E^{*}\) is conserved.

Normalization condition

The Boltzmann distribution must satisfy the normalization condition, which ensures that the sum of probabilities over all microstates equals unity:
Normalization condition
✦
\(\displaystyle\sum_r P_r\) \(=\) \(1\)
Substituting the Boltzmann form
✦
\(C \displaystyle\sum_r e^{{\color{#F08FB0}{-}}\beta E_r}\) \(=\) \(1\)
Normalization constant (partition function)
✦
\(C\) \(=\) \(1\) \(\displaystyle\sum_r e^{{\color{#F08FB0}{-}}\beta E_r}\)

Boltzmann Distribution

The probability of finding the system in a microstate \(r\) with energy \(E_r\) is given by the Boltzmann distribution, normalized by the partition function:
Boltzmann distribution
✦
\(P_r\) \(=\) \(e^{{\color{#F08FB0}{-}}\beta E_r}\) \(\displaystyle\sum_r e^{{\color{#F08FB0}{-}}\beta E_r}\)

Partition Function

The denominator of the Boltzmann distribution defines the canonical partition function \(Z\), which sums over all microstates:
Canonical partition function
✦
\(Z\) \(=\) \(\displaystyle\sum_r e^{{\color{#F08FB0}{-}}\beta E_r}\)

Mean (Expectation) of a Parameter \(y\)

The average value of any observable \(y\) (which takes values \(y_r\) in microstate \(r\)) is computed by weighting each microstate by its Boltzmann probability:
Ensemble average
✦
\(\bar{y}\) \(=\) \(\displaystyle\sum_r P_r y_r\)
Ensemble average
✦
\(\bar{y}\) \(=\) \(\displaystyle\sum_r (e^{{\color{#F08FB0}{-}}\beta E_r}) y_r\) \(\displaystyle\sum_r e^{{\color{#F08FB0}{-}}\beta E_r}\)

Magnetization

Magnetization \(M\) is defined as the net magnetic moment per unit volume. For a system of \(N_0\) non-interacting magnetic moments, the average magnetic moment \(\bar{u}\) determines the total magnetization:
Magnetization from average moment
✦
\(M\) \(=\) \(N_0 {\color{#F08FB0}{\cdot}} \bar{u}\)
For a two-state paramagnet (spin-1/2) in a magnetic field \(B\), the average moment is given by a hyperbolic tangent, leading to:
Magnetization of a two-state paramagnet
✦
\(M\) \(=\) \(N_0 \, \mu_0 \, {\color{#F08FB0}{\tanh}}{\color{#F08FB0}{\bigl(}}\frac{\mu_0 B}{k T}{\color{#F08FB0}{\bigr)}}\)
where
\(\mu_0 B\) - magnetic energy
\(k T\) - thermal energy

Weak-field Limit: Curie's Law

In the weak-field regime, the magnetic energy is much smaller than the thermal energy:
Weak-field condition
✦
\(\mu_0 B\) \(\ll\) \(k T\)
Under this condition, the hyperbolic tangent can be approximated by its argument:
Linear approximation of \(\tanh\)
✦
\({\color{#F08FB0}{\tanh}}{{\color{#F08FB0}{\big (}}\frac{\mu_0 B}{k T}{\color{#F08FB0}{\big )}}}\) \(\approx\) \(\mu_0 B\) \(k T\)
Substituting into the expression for magnetization gives:
Magnetization in weak field
✦
\(M\) \(=\) \(N_0\) \(\cdot\) \(\mu_0^2 B\) \(k T\)
The magnetic susceptibility \(\chi\) is defined as the ratio of magnetization to the applied field (per unit volume). For a sample of volume \(V\):
Definition of susceptibility
✦
\(\chi\) \(=\) \(M\) \(V\)
Inserting the weak-field magnetization yields:
Curie law
✦
\(\chi\) \(=\) \(N_0 \mu_0^2\) \(V k T\)
Thus the susceptibility is inversely proportional to temperature:
Curie's law proportionality
✦
\(\chi\) \(\propto\) \(1\) \(T\)

Mean Energy

The average (mean) energy of a system in the canonical ensemble is given by the weighted sum over microstates, where the weight is the Boltzmann factor:
Ensemble average energy
✦
\(\bar{E}\) \(=\) \(\displaystyle\sum_r (e^{{\color{#F08FB0}{-}}\beta E_r}) \, E_r\) \(\displaystyle\sum_r e^{{\color{#F08FB0}{-}}\beta E_r}\)
Notice that the factor \(e^{-\beta E_r} E_r\) can be expressed as a derivative with respect to \(\beta\):
Derivative identity
✦
\(e^{-\beta E_r} \, E_r\) \(=\) \(-\) \(\displaystyle\frac{\partial}{\partial \beta} \bigl( e^{{\color{#F08FB0}{-}}\beta E_r} \bigr)\)
Substituting this into the expression for \(\bar{E}\) allows us to take the derivative outside the sum:
Derivative of the sum
✦
\(\bar{E}\) \(=\) \(\displaystyle\sum_r {\color{#F08FB0}{-}}\displaystyle\frac{\partial}{\partial \beta} \bigl( e^{{\color{#F08FB0}{-}}\beta E_r} \bigr)\) \(\displaystyle\sum_r e^{{\color{#F08FB0}{-}}\beta E_r}\)
Pulling the derivative out
✦
\(\bar{E}\) \(=\) \({\color{#F08FB0}{-}}\displaystyle\frac{\partial}{\partial \beta} {\color{#F08FB0}{\Big(}} \displaystyle\sum_r e^{{\color{#F08FB0}{-}}\beta E_r} {\color{#F08FB0}{\Big )}}\) \(\displaystyle\sum_r e^{{\color{#F08FB0}{-}}\beta E_r}\)
Recognizing the partition function \(Z = \sum_r e^{-\beta E_r}\), we obtain a compact formula for the mean energy:
Mean energy in terms of \(Z\)
✦
\(\bar{E}\) \(=\) \(-\) \(1\) \(Z\) \(\cdot\) \(\partial Z\) \(\partial \beta\)
✦
✦ Mean Energy
\(\bar{E}\) \(=\) \(-\) \(\displaystyle\frac{\partial}{\partial \beta} \left(\ln{Z}\right)\)

✦ Example 1 finding the mean of magnetic moment
Problem

Consider a paramagnetic material consisting \(N_0\) identical, non-interacting atoms per unit volume. Each atom has spin \(\frac{1}{2}\) and magnetic moment \(\mu_0\). The system is in thermal equilibrium at temperature \(T\) with a heat reservoir \(A'\). Each atom can be treated as an independent system in contact with the reservoir at temperature \(T\). For a single atom, the magnetic moment is \(+\mu_0\) when the spin is aligned with the external magnetic field \(B\) (up state) and \(-\mu_0\) when anti-aligned (down state). Using the canonical ensemble, construct the partition function \(Z\) for a single atom and derive the mean magnetic moment \(\bar{u}\).

Solution

The partition function for a single spin-1/2 atom in a magnetic field is the sum over all microstates of the Boltzmann factor:

\[ Z = \sum_r e^{-\beta E_r} \]

Since the atom has only two states (spin up and spin down), the sum runs from r = 1 to 2:

\[ Z = \sum_{r=1}^2 e^{-\beta E_r} \]

Writing the two terms explicitly for the up and down spin states:

\[ Z = e^{-\beta E_{\text{up}}} + e^{-\beta E_{\text{down}}} \]

The energy of a magnetic moment \(\mu_0\) in a field \(B\) is \(E = -\vec{\mu} \cdot \vec{B}\). For the up state, the magnetic moment is \(+\mu_0\) (aligned with the field), giving energy \(E_{\text{up}} = -\mu_0 B\). For the down state, the magnetic moment is \(-\mu_0\) (anti-aligned with the field), giving energy \(E_{\text{down}} = +\mu_0 B\). Substituting these:

\[ Z = e^{-\beta (-\mu_0 B)} + e^{-\beta (+\mu_0 B)} \]

Simplifying the exponents:

\[ Z = e^{\beta \mu_0 B} + e^{-\beta \mu_0 B} \]

This sum of exponentials can be written in terms of the hyperbolic cosine:

\[ Z = 2 \cosh{(\beta \mu_0 B)} \]

The mean magnetic moment \(\bar{u}\) is calculated by averaging the magnetic moment \(y_r\) over all states using the probability \(P_r = e^{-\beta E_r}/Z\):

\[ \bar{u} = \sum_r P_r y_r \] \[ \bar{u} = \frac{\sum_r e^{-\beta E_r} y_r}{\sum_r e^{-\beta E_r}} \]

For the two-state system, the magnetic moment is \(+\mu_0\) for the up state and \(-\mu_0\) for the down state. Substituting the energies and moments:

\[ \bar{u} = \frac{e^{-\beta (-\mu_0 B)} (+\mu_0) + e^{-\beta (+\mu_0 B)} (-\mu_0)}{e^{-\beta (-\mu_0 B)} + e^{-\beta (+\mu_0 B)}} \] \[ \bar{u} = \frac{e^{\beta \mu_0 B} (+\mu_0) + e^{-\beta \mu_0 B} (-\mu_0)}{e^{\beta \mu_0 B} + e^{-\beta \mu_0 B}} \]

Factoring \(\mu_0\) in the numerator:

\[ \bar{u} = \mu_0 \left( \frac{e^{\beta \mu_0 B} - e^{-\beta \mu_0 B}}{e^{\beta \mu_0 B} + e^{-\beta \mu_0 B}} \right) \]

Recognizing the fraction as \(\tanh(\beta \mu_0 B)\), the mean magnetic moment becomes:

\[ \bar{u} = \mu_0 \tanh{(\beta \mu_0 B)} \]
✦ Example 2 quantum harmonic oscillator
Problem

Consider a quantum harmonic oscillator with Hamiltonian \(H = \frac{P^2}{2m} + \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 x^2\). The system is in thermal equilibrium at temperature \(T\) with a heat reservoir. Using the canonical ensemble, construct the partition function \(Z\) for the oscillator and derive the mean energy \(\bar{E}\).

Solution

The energy eigenvalues of a quantum harmonic oscillator are given by:

\[ E_n = \left(n + \frac{1}{2}\right)\hbar\omega, \quad n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots \]

The canonical partition function is the sum over all microstates of the Boltzmann factor:

\[ Z = \sum_r e^{-\beta E_r} \]

Substituting the energy eigenvalues:

\[ Z = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} e^{-\beta\left(n+\frac{1}{2}\right)\hbar\omega} \]

Factoring out the constant term:

\[ Z = e^{-\beta\frac{\hbar\omega}{2}} \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} e^{-\beta n\hbar\omega} \]

The sum is a geometric series. Using \(\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} x^n = \frac{1}{1-x}\) with \(x = e^{-\beta\hbar\omega}\):

\[ Z = \frac{e^{-\beta\frac{\hbar\omega}{2}}}{1 - e^{-\beta\hbar\omega}} \]

The mean energy is calculated using:

\[ \bar{E} = -\frac{\partial \ln Z}{\partial \beta} \]

Taking the logarithm of \(Z\):

\[ \ln Z = -\frac{\beta\hbar\omega}{2} - \ln\left(1 - e^{-\beta\hbar\omega}\right) \]

Differentiating with respect to \(\beta\):

\[ \bar{E} = -\frac{\partial}{\partial \beta}\left[-\frac{\beta\hbar\omega}{2} - \ln\left(1 - e^{-\beta\hbar\omega}\right)\right] \] \[ \bar{E} = \frac{\hbar\omega}{2} + \frac{\hbar\omega}{e^{\beta\hbar\omega} - 1} \]

Special Cases:

Case 1: Absolute Zero (\(T = 0\), \(\beta \rightarrow \infty\))

As \(\beta \rightarrow \infty\), \(e^{\beta\hbar\omega} \rightarrow \infty\), so the second term vanishes:

\[ \bar{E} = \frac{1}{2}\hbar\omega \]

This is the zero-point energy of the oscillator.

Case 2: High Temperature (\(kT \gg \hbar\omega\))

For high temperatures, \(\beta\hbar\omega = \frac{\hbar\omega}{kT}\) is small. Expanding \(e^{\beta\hbar\omega} \approx 1 + \frac{\hbar\omega}{kT}\):

\[ \bar{E} \approx \frac{\hbar\omega}{2} + \frac{\hbar\omega}{1 + \frac{\hbar\omega}{kT} - 1} \] \[ \bar{E} \approx \frac{\hbar\omega}{2} + kT \]

Since \(kT \gg \hbar\omega\), the zero-point energy \(\frac{\hbar\omega}{2}\) is negligible:

\[ \bar{E} \approx kT \]

The Hamiltonian has two quadratic terms (kinetic and potential), giving two degrees of freedom. According to the equipartition theorem, each degree of freedom contributes \(\frac{1}{2}kT\) to the average energy:

\[ \bar{E} = f \cdot \frac{1}{2}kT = 2 \cdot \frac{1}{2}kT = kT \]

This matches the high-temperature limit derived from the partition function.

✦ Example 3 finding the mean energy of an ideal gas
Problem

Consider an ideal gas of \(N\) identical and non-interacting molecules of mass \(m\), confined to a reactangle box of volume \(V = L_xL_yL_z\). The gas is in thermal equilibrium at temperature \(T\).

a Write down energy eigen values for a single molecule confined in the box.
b Construct canonical partition function (\(Z\))
Solution

Part (a): Energy eigenvalues

For a single particle in a one-dimensional box of length \(L\), the energy eigenvalues are:

\[ E_n = \frac{n^2 h^2}{8 m L^2}, \quad n = 1, 2, 3, \dots \]

For a three-dimensional rectangular box with sides \(L_x, L_y, L_z\), the energy eigenvalues are:

\[ E_{n_x, n_y, n_z} = \frac{h^2}{8m} \left( \frac{n_x^2}{L_x^2} + \frac{n_y^2}{L_y^2} + \frac{n_z^2}{L_z^2} \right) \]

where \(n_x, n_y, n_z = 1, 2, 3, \dots\)
Part (b): Canonical partition function for a single molecule

The canonical partition function is the sum over all microstates of the Boltzmann factor:

\[ Z = \sum_{n_x, n_y, n_z} e^{-\beta E_{n_x, n_y, n_z}} \]

Substituting the energy eigenvalues:

\[ Z = \sum_{n_x=1}^{\infty} \sum_{n_y=1}^{\infty} \sum_{n_z=1}^{\infty} \exp\left[ -\beta \frac{h^2}{8m} \left( \frac{n_x^2}{L_x^2} + \frac{n_y^2}{L_y^2} + \frac{n_z^2}{L_z^2} \right) \right] \]

The sum factorizes into a product of three independent sums:

\[ Z = \left( \sum_{n_x=1}^{\infty} e^{-\beta \frac{h^2}{8m} \frac{n_x^2}{L_x^2}} \right) \left( \sum_{n_y=1}^{\infty} e^{-\beta \frac{h^2}{8m} \frac{n_y^2}{L_y^2}} \right) \left( \sum_{n_z=1}^{\infty} e^{-\beta \frac{h^2}{8m} \frac{n_z^2}{L_z^2}} \right) \]

Define the constant \(\alpha_x = \frac{\beta h^2}{8m L_x^2}\). Then the \(x\)-direction sum is:

\[ Z_x = \sum_{n_x=1}^{\infty} e^{-\alpha_x n_x^2} \]

For a macroscopic box, the energy levels are very closely spaced, so we approximate the sum by an integral. Extend the lower limit to zero:

\[ Z_x \approx \int_0^{\infty} e^{-\alpha_x n_x^2} \, dn_x \]

The Gaussian integral evaluates to:

\[ \int_0^{\infty} e^{-a x^2} dx = \frac{1}{2} \sqrt{\frac{\pi}{a}} \]

Thus,

\[ Z_x = \frac{1}{2} \sqrt{\frac{\pi}{\alpha_x}} \]

Substitute \(\alpha_x = \frac{\beta h^2}{8m L_x^2}\):

\[ Z_x = \frac{1}{2} \sqrt{\frac{\pi \cdot 8m L_x^2}{\beta h^2}} \]

Simplify the square root:

\[ Z_x = \frac{L_x}{h} \sqrt{\frac{2\pi m}{\beta}} \]

Since \(\beta = \frac{1}{k_B T}\),

\[ Z_x = \frac{L_x}{h} \sqrt{2\pi m k_B T} \]

Similarly for the \(y\) and \(z\) directions:

\[ Z_y = \frac{L_y}{h} \sqrt{2\pi m k_B T} \] \[ Z_z = \frac{L_z}{h} \sqrt{2\pi m k_B T} \]

The total single-molecule partition function is the product:

\[ Z = Z_x Z_y Z_z \] \[ Z = \frac{L_x L_y L_z}{h^3} \left( 2\pi m k_B T \right)^{3/2} \]

Since \(V = L_x L_y L_z\),

\[ Z = \frac{V}{h^3} \left( 2\pi m k_B T \right)^{3/2} \]

Average Energy

The average energy of a single molecule is obtained from:

\[ \langle E \rangle = -\frac{\partial \ln Z}{\partial \beta} \]

First compute \(\ln Z\):

\[ \ln Z = \ln V - 3\ln h + \frac{3}{2} \ln(2\pi m) - \frac{3}{2} \ln \beta \]

Differentiate with respect to \(\beta\):

\[ \frac{\partial \ln Z}{\partial \beta} = -\frac{3}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{\beta} \]

Thus,

\[ \langle E \rangle = -\left( -\frac{3}{2\beta} \right) \] \[ \langle E \rangle = \frac{3}{2\beta} \]

Substitute \(\beta = \frac{1}{k_B T}\):

\[ \langle E \rangle = \frac{3}{2} k_B T \]

This result agrees with the equipartition theorem. A molecule in three dimensions has three translational degrees of freedom, each contributing \(\frac{1}{2} k_B T\) to the average energy:

\[ \langle E \rangle = f \cdot \frac{1}{2} k_B T \] \[ \langle E \rangle = 3 \cdot \frac{1}{2} k_B T \] \[ \langle E \rangle = \frac{3}{2} k_B T \]
