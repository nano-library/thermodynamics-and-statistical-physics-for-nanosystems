✨ The density of states quantifies the number of available microstates per unit energy interval. It serves as a central quantity in statistical physics, bridging microscopic configurations with macroscopic thermodynamic properties.
🌸
Microstate \(\Omega(E)\)
Let \(\Omega(E)\) denote the number of microstates with energies between \(E\) and \(E + \delta E\). In other words, \(\Omega(E)\) is the number of states per unit energy interval.
🌸
Number of accessible states per unit energy \(\omega(E)\)
The quantity \(\omega(E)\) is defined as the number of accessible states per unit energy.
In macroscopic systems, the degeneracy of each energy level is enormous, and neighbouring energy levels are extremely closely spaced. For example, the level spacing for air in a typical room is on the order of \(10^{-24}\) eV, which is roughly \(10^{-50}\) of the total internal energy of the air. Such a spacing is far below any measurable resolution. Importantly, \(\omega(E)\) does not depend on the particular choice of \(\delta E\).
Consider a discrete set of energy levels \(E_1, E_2, \ldots, E_n\) with a uniform spacing \(\delta E\).
✦ States in energy interval
✦
\(\Omega(E)\) \(=\) \(\omega(E) \, \delta E\)
For a large system, it is impossible to control the exact quantum energy of the system as a whole. The best we can do is to confine the system to a small energy range \(\delta E\) that is nevertheless very large compared to the typical spacing between individual quantum levels. The number of states lying between \(E\) and \(E + \delta E\) is then given by the above expression.

Functional form of the number of states as a function of energy in a 3D box

Consider a particle of mass \(m\) confined to a three-dimensional box of volume \(V\) with infinitely high potential walls. The Hamiltonian \(H\) of the system is:
✦ Hamiltonian
✦
\(H\) \(=\) \(P_x^2\) \(2m\) \(+\) \(P_y^2\) \(2m\) \(+\) \(P_z^2\) \(2m\) \(+\) \(U\)
Let \(\phi(E)\) denote the number of states with energy less than or equal to \(E\). For a free particle inside the box (where \(U = 0\) inside, infinite outside), the condition that the energy does not exceed \(E\) is:
✦ Energy condition
✦
\(P_x^2 + P_y^2 + P_z^2\) \(2m\) \(\leq\) \(E\)
Multiplying both sides by \(2m\) gives:
✦ Momentum space inequality
✦
\(P_x^2 + P_y^2 + P_z^2\) \(\leq\) \(2mE\)
The right-hand side can be written as the square of a radius:
✦ Sphere radius squared
✦
\(P_x^2 + P_y^2 + P_z^2\) \(\leq\) \({\color{#F08FB0}{\big(}}\sqrt{2mE}{\color{#F08FB0}{\big )}}^2\)
This inequality describes a sphere in three-dimensional momentum space. The number of states \(\phi(E)\) is proportional to the volume of that sphere, hence:
✦ Cumulative states (3D)
✦
\(\phi(E)\) \(\propto\) \(E^{3/2}\)
For a general system,
✦ Generalised scaling
✦
\(\phi {\color{#F08FB0}{(}} E {\color{#F08FB0}{)}}\) \(\propto\) \(E^n\)
The density of states \(\Omega(E)\), the number of states in the small energy interval \([E, E+\delta E]\), is obtained by the difference:
✦ Density of states difference
✦
\(\Omega(E)\) \(=\) \(\phi {\color{#F08FB0}{(}} \, E \, {\color{#F08FB0}{+}} \, \delta E \, {\color{#F08FB0}{)}}\) \(-\) \(\phi {\color{#F08FB0}{(}} E {\color{#F08FB0}{)}}\)
For sufficiently small \(\delta E\), we approximate the difference by the derivative:
✦ Linear approximation
✦
\(\Omega {\color{#F08FB0}{(}} E {\color{#F08FB0}{)}}\) \(\approx\) \(d\phi\) \(dE\) \(\cdot\) \(\delta E\)
Using \(\phi(E) \propto E^{3/2}\) for the three-dimensional box, we differentiate:
✦ Derivative of cumulative states
✦
\(d\phi\) \(dE\) \(\propto\) \(3\) \(2\) \(E^{1/2}\)
Therefore, for a fixed small \(\delta E\), the density of states in a three-dimensional box scales as:
✦ Density of states scaling (3D)
✦
\(\Omega(E)\) \(\propto\) \(E^{1/2}\)

Number of states for an N-particle system in a 3D box

Consider \(N\) particles confined to a three-dimensional potential well. The Hamiltonian, subject to the condition that the total energy does not exceed \(E\), is given by:
✦ Hamiltonian constraint
✦
\(H\) \(=\) \(\displaystyle \sum_{i=1}^{3N}\) \(P_i^2\) \(2m\) \(\leq\) \(E\)
Multiplying both sides by \(2m\) yields:
✦ Momentum sum inequality
✦
\(H\) \(=\) \(\displaystyle \sum_{i=1}^{3N} P_i^2\) \(\leq\) \({\color{#F08FB0}{\big(}}\sqrt{2mE}{\color{#F08FB0}{\big )}}^2\)
This inequality defines a sphere in a \(3N\)-dimensional momentum space. The volume of such a sphere scales with the radius raised to the dimension \(f\), where \(f = 3N\).
Volume scaling (generic)
✦
\(V\) \(\propto\) \(R^f\)
The variable \(f\) denotes the dimensionality of the momentum space. Consequently, the volume scales as:
✦ Volume-energy scaling
✦
\(V\) \(\propto\) \(E^{\frac{f}{2}}\)
For \(f = 3N\), this becomes:
✦ Volume scaling for 3N dimensions
✦
\(V\) \(\propto\) \(E^{\frac{3N}{2}}\)
The cumulative number of states \(\phi(E)\) is proportional to the phase-space volume. Hence:
✦ Cumulative states
✦
\(\phi(E)\) \(\propto\) \(E^{\frac{fN}{2}}\)
Differentiating with respect to energy gives the density of states factor:
✦ Derivative of cumulative states
✦
\(d\phi(E)\) \(dE\) \(\propto\) \(3N\) \(2\) \(E^{3N {\color{#F08FB0}{-}} 1}\)
The number of accessible states \(\Omega(E)\) in an energy interval \(\delta E\) then scales as:
✦ Density of states scaling
✦
\(\Omega(E)\) \(\propto\) \(N\)
Finally, the entropy \(S\) is given by Boltzmann's formula, leading to extensivity:
✦ Entropy scaling
✦
\(S\) \(=\) \(k {\color{#F08FB0}{\ln}} \Omega {\color{#F08FB0}{(}} E {\color{#F08FB0}{)}}\) \(\propto\) \(N\)

General Expression of \(\Omega = f{\color{#F08FB0}{(}} E {\color{#F08FB0}{)}}\)

Consider a macroscopic system with \(f\) degrees of freedom. The total number of accessible states scales as:
Accessible states scaling
✦
\(\Omega {\color{#F08FB0}{(}} E {\color{#F08FB0}{)}}\) \(\propto\) \( {\color{#F08FB0}{(}} \, E \, {\color{#F08FB0}{-}} \, E_0 \, {\color{#F08FB0}{)}}^{\color{#6AAEE0}{f}}\)
Here, \(f \sim 10^{23}\), an astronomically large number characteristic of macroscopic systems.
✦ Logarithm of accessible states
✦
\({\color{#F08FB0}{\ln}} \, \Omega {\color{#F08FB0}{(}} E {\color{#F08FB0}{)}} \) \(\approx\) \(f\) \(\ln\) \({\color{#F08FB0}{(}} \, E \, {\color{#F08FB0}{-}} \, E_0 \, {\color{#F08FB0}{)}}\)
Boltzmann entropy
✦
\(S\) \(=\) \(k\) \(\ln\) \(\Omega {\color{#F08FB0}{(}} E {\color{#F08FB0}{)}} \)
✦ Entropy scaling with f
✦
\(S\) \(\approx\) \(k \, f\) \(\ln \, {\color{#F08FB0}{(}} \, {\color{#6AAEE0}{E}} \, {\color{#F08FB0}{-}} \, {\color{#6AAEE0}{E_0}} \, {\color{#F08FB0}{)}} \)

Dependence of the Number of Accessible Microstates \(\Omega\) on External Constraints

The number of accessible microstates \(\Omega\) depends on external constraints. Typical constraints include:
  • Total energy \(E\): increasing \(E\) expands the available phase space volume, thus increasing \(\Omega\).
  • Volume \(V\): a larger volume allows more spatial configurations, leading to a larger \(\Omega\).
  • Number of particles \(N\): more particles introduce additional degrees of freedom, dramatically increasing \(\Omega\).
  • Position of a partition (sub-volumes): moving a partition changes the accessible volume for each subsystem, thereby altering the total number of microstates.
Let \(y\) denote the set of such constraints. Then \(\Omega\) becomes a function of \(y\):
✦ Functional dependence on constraints
✦
\(\Omega\) \(=\) \(\Omega {\color{#F08FB0}{(}} y {\color{#F08FB0}{)}}\)

Equilibrium and Irreversibility

🌸
Equilibrium
An isolated system is in equilibrium when all its accessible microstates are equally probable.
🌸
Irreversible Direction
When an external constraint is changed (e.g., a change in total energy or volume), the number of accessible microstates in the final state is greater than that in the initial state. Such a process is irreversible.
✦ Irreversible increase
✦
\(\Omega_f\) \(\gg\) \(\Omega_i\)
Consequently, the entropy also increases:
✦ Entropy increase
✦
\(S_f\) \(\approx\) \(\Omega_f\) \(>\) \(S_i\) \(\approx\) \(\Omega_i\)
In the rare case of a reversible process, the entropy remains constant:
✦ Reversible case
✦
\(\Omega_f\) \(=\) \(\Omega_i\)
